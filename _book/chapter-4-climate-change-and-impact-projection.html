Chapter 4 — Climate Change and Impact Projection

This chapter discusses how global warming affects chill accumulation and flowering timing.

Drivers of climate change

List the main drivers of climate change at the decade to century scale, and briefly explain the mechanism through which the currently most important driver affects our climate.

Main drivers at decadal to centennial scales:

  • Greenhouse gases (GHGs) (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O)
  • Aerosols
  • Surface albedo changes
  • Clouds
  • Ozone
  • Solar variability (minor role in recent decades)
  • Volcanic activity (short-term effects)

Currently most important driver: Greenhouse gases

Greenhouse gases trap heat in the Earth’s atmosphere by absorbing long-wave radiation emitted from the Earth’s surface while also allowing short-wave solar radiation to pass through. This enhances the natural greenhouse effect, increasing Earth’s mean temperature. Human activities, especially fossil fuel combustion, cement production, and land-use change, have caused atmospheric CO₂ concentrations to rise faster and to higher levels than ever before in human history, making GHGs the dominant driver of recent climate change.

Recent temperature dynamics and causes for concern

Explain briefly what is special about temperature dynamics of recent decades, and why we have good reasons to be concerned.

Characteristics of recent warming:

  • Rapid and global warming since about 1901, visible almost everywhere on earth
  • The temperature is rising faster than ever before in human history
  • The known “hockey stick” pattern is showing a sharp rise in recent temperatures
  • Temperatures are rising much faster than during past natural climate changes

Why this is concerning:

  • rapid change leave little time for natural and human systems to adapt
  • It increases the risk of positive feedbacks, such as:
    • Melting ice and snow → reduced albedo means more warming
    • Permafrost thaw → methane release → further warming
  • Even “moderate” warming targets (1.5–2°C) represent extreme changes compared to historical variability

Representative Concentration Pathways (RCPs)

What does the abbreviation ‘RCP’ stand for, how are RCPs defined, and what is their role in projecting future climates?

Definition:

RCP stands for Representative Concentration Pathway.

  • RCPs are greenhouse gas concentration scenarios used by the IPCC
  • They are defined by the additional radiative forcing (in W m⁻²) expected by the year 2100
    • Example: RCP2.6, RCP4.5, RCP6.0, RCP8.5
  • The higher the RCP number, the greater the expected warming

Role in climate projections:

  • RCPs provide standardized future forcing pathways
  • They serve as inputs to Global Climate Models (GCMs)
  • They allow scientists to explore different plausible futures rather than predict a single outcome
  • RCPs are the starting point for climate and impact projections

Climate impact projection methods

Briefly describe the 4 climate impact projection methods described in the fourth video.

The four main climate impact projection methods are:

1. Statistical impact models

  • Use statistical relationships between climate variables (e.g. temperature, rainfall) and observed impacts (e.g. crop yield, species presence)
  • Useful for explaining past trends and projecting future impacts
  • Key limitation → assume relationships remain valid in the future (stationarity)

2. Species Distribution Models (SDMs)

  • Relate species occurrence to climatic variables
  • Used to project current and future habitat suitability
  • Results depend strongly on the chosen algorithm
  • Often combined in ensemble modeling to address uncertainty
  • Limited by equilibrium assumptions and missing non-climatic drivers

3. Process-based (mechanistic) models

  • Explicitly simulate system processes using equations
  • Common in agriculture, hydrology, and ecology
  • Can test management and adaptation options
  • Major limitations include:
    • Poor understanding of some processes
    • High parameter uncertainty
    • Risk of imbalance (some processes over-modeled, others ignored)

4. Climate analogue models

  • Identify locations today with climates similar to a site’s projected future climate
  • Use real-world systems to infer future impacts
  • Particularly useful for participatory learning and adaptation planning
  • Limitations include differences in soils, management,and socio-economic conditions
