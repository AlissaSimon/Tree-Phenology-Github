4 Chapter 5 — Winter Chill Projections

In this section, we simulate chill accumulation using temperature data and explore potential future reductions due to climate change.

4.1 Exercise 1

Sketch out three data access and processing challenges that had to be overcome in order to produce chill projections with state-of-the-art methodology

  • Data availability and size
    • Early climate models were hard to access and distributed as large global datasets (hundreds of MB per variable), making them difficult to process on normal computers - Researchers needed location-specific, manageable data (solved later with platforms like ClimateWizard and AFRICLIM)
      • Lack of hourly temperature data
        • Chill models (especially the Dynamic Model) need hourly temperature inputs, but most climate projections provide only daily minimum and maximum values
        • Converting daily data to hourly series required developing interpolation algorithms and weather generators
      • Automation and reproducibility
        • Each study included many stations, multiple models, and several emission scenarios
        • Researchers had to automate workflows for data cleaning, model running, and visualization using programming (e.g. in R), otherwise the workload would be unmanageable ```

4.2 Exercise 2

Outline, in your understanding, the basic steps that are necessary to make such projections

  1. Collect climate data

    Get temperature data from weather stations or climate models for both past and future time periods. Usually, these are daily minimum and maximum temperatures

  2. Convert to hourly data

    Because chill models need hourly temperatures, you have to estimate hourly values from the daily data (using a formula or weather generator)

  3. Calculate chill

    Use a chill model (for example, the Dynamic Model) to calculate how much chill trees get at each location

  4. Compare current and future scenarios

    Do the same calculation for future temperature data and compare how much chill will be lost or gained

  5. Summarize and visualize the results

    Show the results in tables or maps (like Safe Winter Chill maps) to see which areas might lose enough chill for fruit trees in the future

  6. Interpret the results

    Explain what this means for growers — for example, if they might need to change cultivars or move production to cooler areas ```
